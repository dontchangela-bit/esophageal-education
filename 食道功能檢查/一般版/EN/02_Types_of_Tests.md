# Types of Esophageal Function Tests

## Overview

Esophageal function testing encompasses several different types of tests, each with its specific purpose and clinical application. The following sections introduce the basic principles, procedures, and features of each test to help you understand what your physician may order for you.

---

## 1. High-Resolution Manometry (HRM)

### What is it?

High-resolution manometry is the most important test for evaluating esophageal **muscle motor function**. It uses multiple pressure sensors on a thin catheter to record pressure changes at various points along the esophagus during swallowing.

### Basic Principles

- The catheter diameter is approximately 1/8 inch (about 4 mm), roughly the thickness of a large drinking straw
- The catheter has **36 pressure sensors** that provide a complete recording from the pharynx to the stomach
- You will be asked to swallow water 10 times (approximately 5 mL each), and pressure changes are recorded for each swallow
- A computer converts the pressure data into color images for physician interpretation

### Test Procedure

1. A local anesthetic is sprayed into your nasal passage
2. The thin catheter is gently inserted through the nose into the esophagus and down to the stomach
3. You will be asked to swallow water several times
4. The entire test takes approximately **10 to 15 minutes**
5. The catheter is removed immediately after the test is complete

### What Can It Diagnose?

- Achalasia
- Esophageal spasm
- Ineffective esophageal motility
- Lower esophageal sphincter dysfunction
- Other esophageal motility disorders

---

## 2. 24-Hour pH Monitoring

### What is it?

This test uses a pH sensor placed in the lower esophagus to continuously record esophageal acidity changes over **24 hours**, thereby determining whether abnormal acid reflux is present.

### Basic Principles

- A very thin catheter is inserted through the nose and positioned approximately 5 cm above the lower esophageal sphincter
- The sensor continuously measures the pH value within the esophagus
- Data is recorded on a small portable recorder you carry with you
- You need to record meal times, lying-down times, and times when symptoms occur

### Test Procedure

1. A thin catheter is placed through the nose (similar to HRM but thinner)
2. The catheter is secured to the cheek with tape and connected to a recorder worn at the waist
3. You can go home and live normally while wearing the catheter for 24 hours
4. You return to the hospital the next day to have the catheter removed and the recorder retrieved

### What Can It Diagnose?

- Objective evidence of gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD)
- Assessment of the correlation between reflux and symptoms
- Objective evaluation of medication treatment effectiveness

---

## 3. pH-Impedance Monitoring

### What is it?

pH-impedance monitoring is an **advanced version** of 24-hour pH monitoring. In addition to measuring acidity, it uses impedance technology to detect all types of reflux, including acid reflux, weakly acidic reflux, and non-acid reflux.

### Basic Principles

- The catheter is equipped with both pH sensors and impedance sensors
- Impedance sensors can detect the direction of liquid or gas flow within the esophagus
- It can distinguish between swallowing (downward flow) and reflux (upward flow)
- It can detect reflux regardless of whether the refluxate is acidic or non-acidic

### Comparison with Standard pH Monitoring

| Comparison Item | Standard pH Monitoring | pH-Impedance Monitoring |
|----------------|----------------------|------------------------|
| Detects acid reflux | Yes | Yes |
| Detects non-acid reflux | No | Yes |
| Detects gas reflux | No | Yes |
| Determines reflux direction | No | Yes |
| Applicable scenario | General reflux evaluation | Persistent symptoms despite medication |

### What Can It Diagnose?

- All types of reflux (acidic and non-acidic)
- Reflux status in patients currently on medication (as reflux may become non-acidic while on medication)
- Nature of refluxate (liquid, gas, or mixed)

---

## 4. Wireless pH Monitoring (Bravo)

### What is it?

Wireless pH monitoring uses a capsule-sized micro-sensor (called the Bravo capsule) that attaches directly to the esophageal wall, **eliminating the need for a nasal catheter**, allowing you to undergo reflux monitoring under conditions closer to daily life.

### Basic Principles

- The Bravo capsule is approximately the size of a small pill
- It is typically placed during an endoscopy procedure and attached to the lower esophagus
- The capsule wirelessly transmits pH data to a receiver worn at the waist
- It can continuously record for **48 to 96 hours** (longer than traditional catheter monitoring)
- The capsule detaches on its own after several days and is passed in the stool

### Advantages

- **No nasal catheter discomfort**: No nasal tube required; daily activities and eating are closer to normal
- **Longer monitoring duration**: Records 2 to 4 days of data, increasing detection sensitivity
- **Greater patient acceptance**: Many patients find this method more tolerable

### Considerations

- Placement typically requires an endoscopy (sedation may be needed)
- Some individuals may feel the capsule (mild foreign body sensation)
- MRI should not be performed until the capsule has detached
- The cost is typically higher than traditional catheter monitoring

---

## 5. Functional Lumen Imaging Probe (FLIP)

### What is it?

FLIP is a newer esophageal function testing technology that primarily measures the **distensibility** of the esophagus and esophagogastric junction (EGJ) -- that is, the ability of the esophagus to stretch open.

### Basic Principles

- Uses a probe with an inflatable balloon at its tip
- After the balloon is filled with saline, it simultaneously measures pressure and cross-sectional area within the balloon
- Calculates the distensibility index (DI) of the esophagus
- A lower distensibility index indicates that the esophagus is less easily stretched (possible obstruction or excessive muscle tension)

### Common Applications

- Adjunctive diagnosis of achalasia
- Evaluation of esophagogastric junction obstruction
- Assessment of eosinophilic esophagitis (EoE)
- Post-antireflux surgery outcome evaluation
- Correlation with HRM results

### Test Characteristics

- Usually performed under endoscopic sedation
- Short test duration (approximately 5 to 10 minutes)
- Can be used intraoperatively in real time to guide the surgical procedure

## Comprehensive Comparison of All Tests

| Test Name | What It Measures | Test Duration | Discomfort Level | Nasal Catheter Required | Common Indications |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------|-----------------|------------------------|-------------------|
| HRM (Manometry) | Esophageal pressure and peristalsis | 10-15 minutes | Mild | Yes (during test) | Dysphagia, pre-surgical evaluation |
| 24h pH Monitoring | Esophageal acidity | 24 hours | Mild-Moderate | Yes (worn for 24 hours) | Reflux evaluation |
| pH-Impedance Monitoring | All reflux types | 24 hours | Mild-Moderate | Yes (worn for 24 hours) | Medication-refractory reflux |
| Bravo Wireless Monitoring | Esophageal acidity | 48-96 hours | Mild | No | Reflux evaluation (catheter-free) |
| FLIP | Esophageal distensibility | 5-10 minutes | Mild (under sedation) | No | Achalasia, EoE |

### Discomfort Level Description

- **Mild**: Brief foreign body sensation; well-tolerated by most people
- **Mild-Moderate**: Persistent foreign body sensation while the nasal catheter is in place, but does not interfere with daily activities
- Discomfort levels vary among individuals. If you have particular concerns, please discuss them with your physician

---

## How Does the Physician Choose Which Test to Order?

Your physician will determine the most appropriate combination of tests based on your **symptoms, medical history, and prior test results**. The following are common scenarios:

| Your Primary Concern | Tests That May Be Ordered |
|---------------------|--------------------------|
| Dysphagia with normal endoscopy | HRM |
| Reflux symptoms unresponsive to medication | pH-impedance monitoring |
| Pre-surgical evaluation (antireflux surgery) | HRM + pH monitoring |
| Suspected achalasia | HRM (possibly with FLIP) |
| Unexplained chest pain | HRM + pH monitoring |
| Reflux patient who cannot tolerate nasal catheter | Bravo wireless monitoring |

> **Tip**: Sometimes your physician may order a combination of tests to obtain more comprehensive information. The results of each test complement one another, helping your physician make the most accurate diagnosis.
