Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Below is a compilation of the most commonly asked questions from patients and their families to help you better understand minimally invasive esophageal cancer surgery.

Q1: How long does the minimally invasive surgery take?

Minimally invasive esophagectomy (MIE) typically takes approximately 4-6 hours, depending on the tumor location, size, surgical approach (e.g., Ivor Lewis or McKeown), and whether robot assistance (RAMIE) is used.

Although the operative time is similar to traditional open surgery, postoperative recovery is significantly faster with MIE. From entering the operating room to leaving the recovery room, the entire process takes approximately 6-8 hours. Family members waiting should not be overly worried.

Q2: How long is the hospital stay?

The average hospital stay for minimally invasive surgery is 7-10 days, with a median of approximately 8 days, including: - ICU monitoring for 1-2 days - General ward recovery for 5-6 days

By comparison, traditional open surgery typically requires a hospital stay of 10-14 days. However, recovery speed varies from patient to patient, and your physician will determine the optimal discharge time based on your condition.

Q3: Will a feeding tube be needed after surgery?

Yes. A jejunostomy feeding tube will be placed after surgery. This is a thin, soft tube that connects directly from the abdominal wall to the small intestine, providing nutrition while you are unable to eat normally.

  • Duration of use: Typically 6-8 weeks
  • As oral food intake gradually increases, use of the feeding tube will decrease
  • Removal is an outpatient procedure that is simple and causes minimal discomfort
  • During the home recovery period, nurses and dietitians will teach you and your family how to care for and use the tube

Q4: Will I be able to eat normally after surgery?

Dietary changes are long-term after surgery, but most people can gradually return to a near-normal diet:

  • Short-term (1-2 months postop): Gradual progression from liquids to soft foods, small frequent meals
  • Medium-term (3-6 months postop): Most soft foods can be eaten, but portions are smaller than before surgery
  • Long-term: Most patients can eat the majority of foods, but will need to:
    • Continue with small, frequent meals (5-6 small meals per day)
    • Chew thoroughly and eat slowly
    • Avoid lying down immediately after large meals
    • Some individuals may need to avoid very sweet or fatty foods

Although eating patterns will change, your body will gradually adapt. A dietitian will provide professional guidance throughout the recovery process.

Q5: Will I need chemotherapy or radiation after surgery?

This depends on your situation:

  • Patients who received preoperative chemoradiation: Your physician will decide whether additional postoperative treatment is needed based on the surgical pathology report
  • Adjuvant therapy:
    • If the pathology report shows residual cancer cells in lymph nodes, adjuvant chemotherapy may be recommended
    • In recent years, immunotherapy has also shown promising results in postoperative esophageal cancer treatment
  • Per NCCN 2025 guidelines, treatment plans are tailored by the multidisciplinary team (MDT) to each individual

Q6: What is the survival rate for esophageal cancer?

Survival rates for esophageal cancer are influenced by stage, treatment approach, and hospital experience:

  • Early stage (Stage I): Five-year survival rate can reach 70-80%
  • Intermediate stage (Stage II-III): With complete neoadjuvant chemoradiation plus surgery, three-year survival is approximately 50-60%
    • The MIRO trial showed a three-year overall survival (3-year OS) of 58.4% for MIE
  • Advanced stage (Stage IV): Primarily treated with systemic therapy; survival rate is lower

Important Concept: Survival rates are population-level statistics; every patient’s prognosis is different. Early detection, complete treatment, and selecting an experienced medical team are all key factors in improving outcomes.

Q7: What are the risks of surgery?

All surgeries carry risks. Possible complications of esophagectomy include:

Common Complications

  • Anastomotic leak: Leakage at the esophageal-gastric connection site; incidence approximately 5-15% in general centers, as low as 2-5% in high-volume centers
    • Experienced centers can keep the leak rate low (e.g., 2-5%)
  • Pulmonary complications: Pneumonia, atelectasis, etc.
    • MIE can reduce pulmonary complications by 14-65%
  • Arrhythmia: Common after surgery; most cases are treatable

Rare but Serious Complications

  • Hemorrhage
  • Chylothorax
  • Vocal cord paralysis
  • Wound infection

Operative Mortality

  • High-volume hospitals (25+ cases/year): 3-8%
  • Low-volume hospitals: As high as 16-23%

Choosing a high-volume, experienced medical team is the single most important factor in reducing surgical risk.

Q8: When can I return to work after surgery?

Recovery time varies by individual. General recommendations:

  • Office/desk work: May begin part-time at 6-8 weeks postop, gradually returning to full-time by 8-12 weeks
  • Light physical labor: Approximately 3 months postop
  • Heavy physical labor: Approximately 4-6 months postop, requires physician clearance

It is advisable to discuss flexible work arrangements with your employer beforehand; you may initially need reduced hours or modified duties.

Q9: Is robotic surgery better than conventional MIE?

Robot-assisted minimally invasive esophagectomy (RAMIE) and conventional thoracoscopic/laparoscopic MIE each have advantages:

Feature Conventional MIE RAMIE
Surgical precision Excellent Superior (3D vision, flexible robotic arms)
Learning curve Moderate Steeper initially but stabilizes later
Operative time Shorter Slightly longer
Cost Lower Higher (robotic consumables)
Oncologic outcome Good Good (lymph node harvest may be more thorough)
Availability More widely available Requires specific robotic equipment at medical centers

Current research shows that both approaches have comparable oncologic outcomes. The most important factor remains the experience of the surgical team rather than the specific tool used.

Q10: How should I choose the right hospital and surgeon?

When selecting a hospital, the following points are especially important:

  1. Surgical volume: At least 25 esophagectomies per year is the basic threshold; hospitals performing 60 or more per year achieve the best outcomes
  2. MIE proportion: A higher proportion of minimally invasive procedures typically reflects greater team experience
  3. Multidisciplinary team: Availability of a complete team including surgeons, medical oncologists, radiation oncologists, dietitians, and others
  4. Postoperative support: ICU quality, complication management capabilities
  5. Publicly available data: Some hospitals publish their surgical volumes and outcomes

Questions to Ask Your Surgeon:

  • How many esophagectomies do you personally perform each year?
  • What are your hospital’s anastomotic leak and mortality rates?
  • What minimally invasive technique do you use?
  • What is the composition of the postoperative care team?

Q11: Are the side effects of preoperative chemotherapy or radiation severe?

Side effects of neoadjuvant therapy vary from person to person:

Common Chemotherapy Side Effects

  • Nausea and vomiting (can be controlled with medications)
  • Fatigue
  • Decreased appetite
  • Low white blood cell counts (increased infection risk)
  • Hair loss (depends on the specific drugs)

Common Radiation Therapy Side Effects

  • Esophagitis (pain when swallowing)
  • Fatigue
  • Skin changes in the radiation field

Most side effects gradually improve after treatment ends. Your medical team will provide medications and supportive care to alleviate discomfort.

Q12: Can esophageal cancer recur?

Esophageal cancer can recur, which is why long-term follow-up is essential:

  • Locoregional recurrence: Near the surgical site
  • Distant metastasis: Cancer cells spreading to the liver, lungs, bones, or other organs

How to Reduce Recurrence Risk

  • Complete all recommended preoperative and postoperative treatments
  • Attend regular follow-up visits (at least 5 years)
  • Maintain a healthy lifestyle (quit smoking, balanced diet, moderate exercise)
  • Seek medical attention immediately for any new symptoms

Q13: Will I need to permanently change my eating habits after surgery?

Dietary adjustments after esophageal surgery are indeed long-term, but most people can enjoy the majority of foods:

Long-Term Dietary Principles

  • Small, frequent meals: This is typically a permanent change because the stomach capacity is reduced
  • Chew thoroughly and eat slowly: Develop the habit of eating slowly
  • Avoid carbonated beverages: They easily cause bloating
  • Limit sweets: To prevent dumping syndrome
  • Do not lie down immediately after eating: To prevent reflux

Good News

  • Most patients can eat the majority of foods within 6-12 months
  • The body gradually adapts to the new digestive tract anatomy
  • With dietitian guidance, you can find the dietary pattern that works best for you

Q14: Are there patient support groups?

Yes, you can seek the following support resources:

  • Within the hospital: Many medical centers have cancer patient support groups
  • Cancer Hope Foundation: Provides free counseling and support services
  • Taiwan Cancer Foundation: Provides educational information and support
  • Online communities: Some patients form mutual-support groups on social media

Connecting with patients who have had similar experiences can provide practical tips and emotional support.

Q15: If I do not want surgery, are there other options?

For patients who are not suitable for or do not wish to undergo surgery, other treatment options include:

  • Definitive chemoradiation therapy (dCRT)
    • Primary treatment using concurrent chemoradiation without surgery
    • Suitable for certain cervical esophageal cancers or patients unfit for surgery
    • Studies show therapeutic effectiveness for squamous cell carcinoma
  • Endoscopic treatment for early-stage esophageal cancer
    • Endoscopic mucosal resection (EMR)
    • Endoscopic submucosal dissection (ESD)
    • Only suitable for very early-stage (Stage 0 or select Stage I) esophageal cancer

Recommendation: The choice of treatment should be thoroughly discussed with your medical team. Your physician will provide the most appropriate individualized treatment recommendation based on tumor stage, location, your physical condition, and your preferences.

Q16: Is immunotherapy available for esophageal cancer now?

Yes. In late 2025, the U.S. FDA approved the immunotherapy drug durvalumab (Imfinzi) for early-stage esophageal cancer, making it the first immune checkpoint inhibitor approved for use before and after surgery.

  • Eligible patients: Esophageal and esophagogastric junction adenocarcinoma patients with positive PD-L1 testing
  • Administration: Used in combination with chemotherapy (FLOT regimen), given before and after surgery
  • Efficacy: Clinical trials demonstrated improved event-free survival
  • Cost: Coverage under Taiwan’s National Health Insurance is still being evaluated; partial out-of-pocket costs may apply

The addition of immunotherapy represents a major advance in esophageal cancer treatment. Discuss with your attending physician whether this option is appropriate for you.
