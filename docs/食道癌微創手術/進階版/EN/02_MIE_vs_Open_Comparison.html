Surgical Approach Comparison: MIE vs Open vs RAMIE

Overview of Surgical Approaches

Esophagectomy can be classified by surgical approach and degree of invasiveness:

Surgical Approaches

Approach Route Indications Features
Ivor Lewis (transabdominal-transthoracic) Abdomen + right chest Mid- to lower-esophageal tumors Most commonly used; intrathoracic anastomosis
McKeown (three-incision) Right chest + abdomen + neck Upper to mid-esophageal tumors Cervical anastomosis; allows three-field lymph node dissection
Transhiatal Abdomen + neck Lower esophageal/GEJ tumors Avoids thoracic operation; reduces pulmonary complications
Left thoracoabdominal Left chest-abdomen Lower esophageal/GEJ tumors Single-incision approach; currently less commonly used

Classification by Degree of Invasiveness

Type Definition
Open Traditional open thoracotomy/laparotomy
Hybrid MIE Laparoscopic abdominal phase + open thoracic phase (or vice versa)
Total MIE Thoracoscopic + laparoscopic
RAMIE Robot-assisted thoracic and/or abdominal phases
Single-port MIE Entire surgery completed through a single incision

Key Clinical Trial Evidence

MIRO Trial (France, 2019)

The MIRO trial is the landmark randomized controlled trial (RCT) comparing hybrid MIE with open esophagectomy:

Endpoint Hybrid MIE (n=103) Open (n=104) p value
Primary endpoint: 30-day major complications 35.9% 64.4% < 0.001
Pulmonary complications 17.5% 30.8% 0.037
R0 resection rate Comparable Comparable NS
Lymph node harvest 18 nodes 15 nodes -
3-year overall survival (3-yr OS) 58.4% Comparable NS
Postoperative ICU days Shorter Longer < 0.05

Conclusion: Hybrid MIE significantly reduced major postoperative complications, especially pulmonary complications, without compromising oncologic outcomes.

TIME Trial (Netherlands, 2012)

Comparing total MIE (thoracoscopic + laparoscopic) with open esophagectomy:

Endpoint Total MIE (n=59) Open (n=56) Result
Intraoperative blood loss 200 mL 475 mL Significantly less for MIE
Pulmonary infection 12% 34% Significantly lower for MIE
Hospital stay 11 days 14 days Shorter for MIE
QoL at 6 weeks postop Significantly better - MIE advantage

ROBOT Trial (Netherlands, 2019)

Comparing RAMIE with open esophagectomy:

Endpoint RAMIE (n=54) Open (n=55) p value
Total postoperative complications (Clavien-Dindo ≥ II) 59% 80% 0.02
Pulmonary complications 32% 58% 0.005
Median days to functional recovery 11 days 14 days -
Pain score (postop Day 14) Significantly lower - < 0.05
R0 resection rate Comparable Comparable NS

Volume-Outcome Relationship

Esophagectomy is one of the surgical procedures in which the association between volume and outcomes is strongest:

Hospital Surgical Volume and Mortality

Annual surgical volume Postoperative mortality Major complication rate Failure to rescue (FTR)
< 5 cases 16-23% High High
5-24 cases 8-12% Moderate Moderate
25-59 cases 5-8% Moderate-low Moderate-low
≥ 60 cases 3-5% Low Low

Key Volume-Outcome Indicators

  • High-volume threshold: Internationally recommended as 25 or more cases per year
  • Optimal outcome threshold: 60 or more cases per year
  • Volume affects not only mortality, but also:
    • Early identification and management of complications (failure to rescue)
    • Length of hospital stay
    • Readmission rate
    • Long-term survival

Individual Surgeon Volume

  • Individual surgeon annual volume also affects outcomes
  • Recommended personal annual volume: at least 10-15 cases
  • High-volume surgeons achieve more complete mastery of the learning curve

Learning Curve

Conventional MIE

  • Generally requires completion of 30-50 cases to reliably pass the learning curve
  • Primary challenges: thoracoscopic lymph node dissection, anastomotic technique
  • Training under mentorship at a high-volume center is recommended

RAMIE

  • Learning curve approximately 20-30 cases (some studies suggest shorter)
  • The robotic system’s intuitive controls and 3D visualization help shorten the learning curve
  • Additional training in robotic system operation and troubleshooting is required
  • Structured robotic surgery training programs are recommended

Factors Affecting the Learning Curve

  • Prior open esophageal surgery experience
  • Baseline laparoscopic/thoracoscopic skills
  • Degree of case volume centralization
  • Mentor experience
  • Availability of a structured training program

Conversion from MIE to Open Surgery

Conversion Rates

  • Conventional MIE: 5-15%
  • RAMIE: 3-10%
  • Rates decrease with increasing experience

Common Reasons for Conversion

  1. Severe adhesions
  2. Uncontrollable intraoperative bleeding
  3. Tumor invasion beyond preoperative expectations
  4. Anatomical variation or identification difficulties
  5. Equipment malfunction (especially RAMIE)

Clinical Significance of Conversion

  • Timely conversion to open surgery is a safe and responsible decision and should not be considered a “failure”
  • Delayed conversion may lead to serious complications
  • Patients should be fully informed of the possibility of conversion preoperatively

Special Considerations

Situations Favoring RAMIE

  • Obese patients (BMI > 30)
  • Post-chemoradiation fibrotic tissue
  • Precise lymph node dissection in a narrow operative field
  • Upper esophageal tumors (McKeown approach)

Contraindications or Relative Contraindications to MIE

  • T4b tumors invading the aorta or trachea
  • Severe thoracic adhesions
  • Inability to tolerate one-lung ventilation
  • Certain patients with severe cardiopulmonary disease

ROBOT-2 Trial (Ongoing)

Item Details
Design Multicenter randomized controlled superiority trial
Comparison RAMIE vs MIE (thoracoscopic/laparoscopic minimally invasive)
Population Resectable esophageal or esophagogastric junction adenocarcinoma (cT1-4a, N0-3, M0)
Sample size 218 patients (RAMIE 109 vs MIE 109)
Primary endpoint Total lymph node harvest per TIGER classification
Secondary endpoints Surgical outcomes, complications, survival, cost-effectiveness
Hypothesis RAMIE can achieve superior lymph node harvest
Status Enrollment ongoing; results expected in 2026-2027

This trial will provide the first large-scale RCT evidence comparing RAMIE vs MIE and is expected to clarify the role of robotic surgery in esophageal cancer treatment.

Future Directions

  1. Single-port MIE: National Taiwan University Hospital (NTUH) has pioneering experience
  2. ICG fluorescence-guided surgery: Anastomotic blood flow assessment, lymph node mapping
  3. AI-assisted surgery: Real-time intraoperative image recognition
  4. Augmented reality: Intraoperative anatomical structure localization
  5. Next-generation robotic platforms: Ion system, etc.
  6. ERAS (Enhanced Recovery After Surgery): Standardized implementation of enhanced recovery protocols
