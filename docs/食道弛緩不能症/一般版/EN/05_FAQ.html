Esophageal Achalasia — Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

About the Disease

Q1: Is esophageal achalasia cancer?

No. Esophageal achalasia is an esophageal motility disorder, not cancer.

However, patients with long-term untreated disease — due to persistent esophageal inflammation and dilation — may have a slightly increased risk of developing esophageal cancer over time. Therefore, appropriate treatment and regular endoscopic follow-up are recommended.

Additionally, during the diagnostic workup, your physician will specifically rule out “pseudoachalasia,” because tumors at the lower esophagus can sometimes produce symptoms similar to achalasia. This is one reason why upper endoscopy is always performed during diagnosis.

Q2: Is this disease hereditary?

Current medical research indicates that esophageal achalasia is not a typical hereditary disease. The vast majority of patients have no family members with this condition.

Although familial clustering has been observed in extremely rare cases, no definitive pathogenic gene has been identified. Therefore, your family members need not be particularly concerned about inheriting this disease.

If you have serious concerns, genetic counseling may be considered.

Q3: Can children develop esophageal achalasia?

Yes, but it is very rare. The incidence of achalasia in children is far lower than in adults, accounting for fewer than 5% of all cases.

Symptoms in children may differ from those in adults, and common presentations include: - Slow eating - Vomiting - Recurrent respiratory infections - Failure to thrive - Nighttime coughing

If a child has persistent symptoms as described above, evaluation by a pediatric gastroenterologist is recommended. Treatment approaches are similar to those for adults but require assessment and management by a pediatric specialist.

Q4: Can esophageal achalasia be completely cured?

Currently, it cannot be completely cured. The underlying cause — degeneration of ganglion cells within the esophagus — is irreversible.

However, the good news is that with appropriate treatment (such as POEM, Heller myotomy, or pneumatic dilation), the vast majority of patients can achieve significant improvement in dysphagia and return to near-normal eating and daily life.

Think of it like nearsightedness: it cannot be “cured,” but with glasses or laser surgery, your vision can be restored to a functional level. The treatment concept for achalasia is similar.

Q5: Is this disease related to stress or emotions?

The development of esophageal achalasia has no direct causal relationship with stress or emotions. It is an organic disease with objective test abnormalities, not a condition caused by psychological factors.

That said, stress and anxiety can temporarily worsen the sensation of difficulty swallowing, as emotional tension may trigger esophageal spasm.

If you have been diagnosed with achalasia, please do not blame yourself — this is not something you caused by “overthinking” or “being too stressed.”

About Treatment

Q6: Is POEM surgery safe?

POEM (Peroral Endoscopic Myotomy) is a safe and effective treatment when performed at experienced medical centers.

Safety data: - Serious complication rate is low, approximately 1–3% - Common minor complications include pneumoperitoneum and mild bleeding, which usually resolve without additional intervention - Serious complications such as esophageal perforation are very rare - Tens of thousands of successful cases have been performed worldwide

Possible post-operative issues: - The most frequently discussed concern is post-operative GERD, which occurs in approximately 20–50% of patients; most cases can be managed with medication - Symptom relief rate is as high as 80–95%

Key point: Choosing an experienced medical team is very important. The efficacy and safety of POEM are closely related to the physician’s experience and case volume.

Q7: Can symptoms recur after treatment?

Yes, but the likelihood varies by treatment method:

Treatment Likelihood of Recurrence Details
POEM Low Approximately 5–15% may need retreatment within 5 years
Heller Myotomy Low Approximately 15–20% may see symptom decline at 10 years
Pneumatic Dilation Moderate Approximately 25–35% require repeat dilation within 5 years
BTX Injection High Nearly all patients experience recurrence after 6–12 months

If symptoms recur: - Do not be overly worried; your doctor has multiple retreatment options - Repeat dilation, POEM, or alternative approaches may be used - Recurrence does not mean the initial treatment “failed”; sometimes multi-stage management is needed

Q8: Can I eat normally after treatment?

Most patients can resume near-normal eating after treatment.

  • During the first few weeks after treatment, a gradual dietary recovery plan should be followed (liquids → soft foods → regular diet)
  • After recovery, most patients can enjoy a variety of foods
  • It is still recommended to maintain good habits: small bites, thorough chewing, drinking water with meals, and avoiding large meals before bed
  • A small number of patients may still have some difficulty with very hard or very dry foods

General patient experience: Many patients report that the most rewarding aspect of treatment is “finally being able to dine normally with family and friends again.” While some attention to eating techniques may still be needed, overall quality of life improves significantly.

Q9: How should I choose between Heller myotomy and POEM?

This is one of the most frequently asked questions. A brief comparison:

Category POEM Heller Myotomy
External incision None (transoral) 3–5 small incisions
Post-op reflux risk Higher (20–50%) Lower (10–30%, with fundoplication)
Hospital stay 1–2 days 1–3 days
Symptom relief rate 80–95% 85–95%
Efficacy for Type III Particularly good More limited

Factors to consider: - If your subtype is Type III (spastic), POEM is usually the better choice - If you are very concerned about post-operative reflux, Heller + fundoplication may be more appropriate - The physician’s experience and expertise are also important considerations - The final decision should be discussed in detail with your attending physician

Per the SAGES 2024 guidelines, POEM has been conditionally recommended over pneumatic dilation and is specifically recommended for Type III achalasia.

Q10: Will I need lifelong medication after treatment?

Usually not.

  • Treatment aims to resolve the problem through physical means (cutting or dilating the sphincter), so no medications are needed to maintain the effect
  • However, if post-operative GERD develops, proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) may be needed to control gastric acid
  • PPIs are typically used short- to medium-term; some patients may need long-term use
  • Apart from reflux-controlling medication, no other long-term drugs are required

About Daily Life

Q11: Can I exercise?

Yes, and moderate exercise is recommended.

  • General physical activities (walking, jogging, swimming, yoga, etc.) are beneficial
  • Avoid vigorous exercise immediately after meals
  • Avoid movements that significantly increase abdominal pressure (e.g., Valsalva maneuver during weight training, intense sit-ups), as they may worsen reflux
  • In the first few weeks after treatment, follow your doctor’s instructions regarding exercise intensity

Q12: Will pregnancy be affected?

Esophageal achalasia does not affect fertility. However, certain considerations apply during pregnancy:

  • Pregnancy itself may worsen dysphagia symptoms (due to hormonal changes + increased abdominal pressure)
  • It is recommended to complete treatment before becoming pregnant
  • If symptoms worsen during pregnancy, management should be discussed jointly with the obstetrician and gastroenterologist
  • BTX injection is generally not recommended during pregnancy

Q13: What if symptoms return after treatment?

There is no need for excessive anxiety. Follow these steps:

  1. Observe first: occasional mild swallowing difficulty may be normal fluctuation
  2. Adjust your diet: return to basic techniques such as small bites, thorough chewing, and drinking water with meals
  3. Schedule a follow-up visit: inform your doctor about symptom changes
  4. Re-evaluation: your doctor may order a barium study or manometry to assess the situation
  5. Retreatment options: repeat dilation, another POEM, or other interventions may be considered as needed

Important: Symptom recurrence does not mean there is no solution. Modern medicine has multiple management strategies, and your doctor will select the most appropriate approach for you.

Q14: Will insurance cover the treatment costs?

  • In Taiwan, diagnosis and most treatment options for achalasia (including pneumatic dilation and Heller myotomy) are typically covered by the National Health Insurance (NHI)
  • POEM may involve out-of-pocket costs or material surcharges at some hospitals
  • Before treatment, it is advisable to inquire at the hospital’s social work office or medical billing consultation desk for detailed cost information
  • Patients who qualify for catastrophic illness certification may apply to the NHI Administration

Reminder: Billing practices and out-of-pocket items vary by hospital; please confirm in advance.

Q15: Are there patient support groups or resources available?

  • Currently, there is no dedicated achalasia patient support group in Taiwan, but relevant discussion communities can be found on social media
  • Multiple English-language support groups and online forums exist internationally
  • Hospital social workers can provide emotional support and resource referrals
  • If emotional distress is significant, consider consulting a psychologist or psychiatrist
