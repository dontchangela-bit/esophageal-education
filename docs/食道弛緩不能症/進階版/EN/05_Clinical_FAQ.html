Esophageal Achalasia — Clinical FAQ

Subtypes and Treatment Selection

Q1: Which subtype responds best to POEM?

Type II (panesophageal pressurization) has the best treatment response, with the highest success rates among all three subtypes for all treatment modalities (POEM, LHM, PD). POEM success rate can reach 90–95%.

The reason is that Type II patients retain some degree of esophageal wall muscle function (manifested as the panesophageal pressurization phenomenon). Once LES resistance is removed by POEM, the intraluminal pressure can effectively propel food through.

Type III (spastic), although it responds poorly to conventional treatments (PD, LHM), responds remarkably well to POEM (85–95%), because POEM can extend the myotomy proximally to > 10 cm along the esophageal body, covering the spastic segment. This is the primary basis for the SAGES 2024 recommendation of POEM as first-line for Type III.

Type I (classic) typically indicates longer disease duration with significant esophageal dilation; treatment response is intermediate (POEM success rate approximately 80–90%).

Q2: When should pneumatic dilation (PD) be chosen over myotomy (POEM/LHM)?

Scenarios favoring PD over POEM/LHM include:

Scenario Rationale
Patient strongly wishes to avoid general anesthesia PD can be performed under conscious sedation
Limited medical resources without POEM/LHM availability PD has a lower technical threshold
Pregnant women needing temporary relief Considerations regarding general anesthesia risk
Bridge therapy while awaiting definitive treatment Provides symptom relief
Patient’s condition unsuitable for prolonged surgery PD has a shorter procedure time

However, SAGES 2024 has conditionally recommended POEM over PD, primarily based on POEM’s more durable medium-term efficacy and lower retreatment rate. When the patient is suitable for and has access to POEM, it is generally recommended as the preferred option.

Graded Dilation Protocol for PD: - First session: 30 mm balloon - If inadequate response, repeat with 35 mm balloon after 4–6 weeks - If necessary, further dilation with 40 mm balloon - Perforation must be ruled out before each dilation

Q3: How should post-POEM GERD be managed?

The incidence of post-POEM GERD (20–50%) is one of the most discussed clinical concerns. Management strategy:

Routine post-operative assessment: - 24-hour pH monitoring (or pH-impedance monitoring) recommended at 3–12 months post-op - Endoscopic assessment of esophageal mucosa (Los Angeles Classification) recommended at 1 year post-op - Objective assessment is recommended even in asymptomatic patients (approximately 15–30% have asymptomatic abnormal acid exposure)

Pharmacological management: - For symptomatic patients or those with objectively confirmed abnormal acid exposure: initiate PPI therapy - Common medications: Omeprazole 20 mg, Esomeprazole 40 mg, Pantoprazole 40 mg, etc., once daily - Most patients are effectively controlled with standard-dose PPI - Some patients may require long-term use

Management of refractory GERD: - Double the PPI dose - Add an H2 receptor antagonist (H2RA) at bedtime - Evaluate whether other causes may be contributing (e.g., poor esophageal emptying causing symptoms) - In rare refractory cases, anti-reflux surgery (e.g., fundoplication) may be considered, but the risks of reoperation must be weighed

Long-term monitoring: - Endoscopic follow-up every 1–3 years to monitor for Barrett’s esophagus development - Data on Barrett’s esophagus incidence beyond 10 years post-POEM are still accumulating

Q4: What options are available after initial treatment failure?

Treatment failure is generally defined as Eckardt Score > 3 or lack of improvement on objective testing (TBE, HRM).

Systematic evaluation after failure: 1. Confirm the diagnosis is correct (rule out pseudoachalasia) 2. Assess whether the initial treatment was adequate (myotomy length, dilation degree) 3. Evaluate esophageal morphological changes (progression to sigmoid esophagus) 4. Consider whether fibrosis or scarring has developed

Second-line treatment options:

Initial Treatment Recommended Retreatment Options Success Rate Notes
PD failure POEM or LHM 70–85% Both have good evidence
POEM failure Repeat POEM (different direction), PD, or LHM 60–80% Posterior POEM can be switched to anterior, or vice versa
LHM failure POEM (posterior approach) or PD 60–75% POEM can avoid the anterior surgical field
Multiple failures Consider esophagectomy — Last resort; higher surgical risk

Special advantages of POEM as salvage therapy (Redo POEM): - Can choose a different entry direction from the prior procedure (anterior vs posterior) - The submucosal tunnel approach can bypass prior surgical scar tissue - Technically more challenging; recommended at high-volume centers

Diagnosis and Evaluation

Q5: What is the role of FLIP (Functional Lumen Imaging Probe) in achalasia?

FLIP is an esophageal function assessment tool based on impedance planimetry that measures:

  • EGJ Distensibility Index (EGJ-DI): significantly reduced in achalasia patients
  • Esophageal body contraction patterns (FLIP Panometry): identifies the presence and pattern of peristalsis

Clinical applications: | Application | Role | |————-|——| | Inconclusive HRM results | Adjunctive diagnosis providing additional functional information | | EGJOO differentiation | Helps distinguish true early achalasia from functional EGJOO | | Intraoperative assessment | Real-time assessment of myotomy adequacy during POEM or LHM | | Post-operative follow-up | Objective evaluation of EGJ patency improvement | | Research | Further understanding of achalasia pathophysiology |

Current positioning: - FLIP is not yet listed as a routine diagnostic tool, but its value in special scenarios is increasingly recognized - Intraoperative FLIP use is growing; studies suggest it may improve surgical success rates - The EAES 2026 guideline is expected to provide more specific recommendations on the role of FLIP

Q6: When and why should manometry follow-up be performed?

Significance of post-treatment HRM follow-up: - Objective assessment of whether LES pressure has decreased - Evaluation of IRP changes (one objective indicator of treatment success) - Note: post-treatment HRM interpretation differs from pre-treatment; a divided sphincter may not display a “normal” manometric pattern

Recommended follow-up timing:

Time Point HRM Follow-Up Recommendation
Within 3 months post-op Generally not recommended (esophagus is still recovering)
3–6 months post-op May be considered if symptoms have not improved
1 year post-op Recommended as an objective efficacy assessment
When symptoms recur Should be performed for re-evaluation

HRM follow-up considerations: - Post-treatment IRP falling within the normal range is a good indicator of success - However, some clinically successful patients (with symptom improvement) may still have IRP slightly above the upper limit of normal - Assessment should integrate symptoms (Eckardt Score) with objective testing (TBE, HRM)

Q7: How should sigmoid esophagus be managed?

Sigmoid esophagus is the result of long-term achalasia progression, with severe esophageal dilation and S-shaped curvature.

Grading (by severity):

Grade Esophageal Diameter Esophageal Morphology
Mild dilation < 4 cm Straight or slight curvature
Moderate dilation 4–6 cm Possible mild curvature
Severe dilation (megaesophagus) > 6 cm Pronounced S-shaped curvature

Treatment strategy:

Severity Treatment Recommendation Expected Outcome
Mild to moderate POEM or LHM can still be attempted Success rate slightly lower than non-dilated cases, but most are still effective
Severe with residual function POEM/LHM + close follow-up May require multiple interventions
Severe with loss of function Consider esophagectomy Higher surgical risk, but provides a definitive solution

Special challenges of POEM/LHM in sigmoid esophagus: - Increased technical difficulty for endoscopic or laparoscopic operation - Abnormal esophageal course may affect tunnel creation (POEM) - Even with reduced sphincter pressure, the dilated esophagus may still have poor emptying function - Should be performed at high-volume centers by experienced operators

Special Populations

Q8: Safety and efficacy of POEM in elderly patients?

Overall, POEM remains safe and effective in elderly patients (> 65–70 years).

Category Elderly Group (> 65 years) Non-Elderly Group
Symptom relief rate 80–90% 85–95%
Serious complication rate Slightly higher (3–5%) 1–3%
Hospital stay May be slightly longer (2–4 days) 1–3 days
Post-operative GERD Similar incidence Similar
Overall safety Acceptable Good

Special considerations for elderly patients: - Preoperative cardiopulmonary assessment is more critical - Risk of general anesthesia requires careful evaluation - If general anesthesia risk is too high, consider PD (under sedation) or BTX injection - Treatment goals may focus more on symptom relief and nutritional maintenance rather than pursuing perfect functional restoration

Q9: Can medications used by achalasia patients affect esophageal function?

Medications requiring attention:

Drug Class Effect on Esophageal Function Recommendation
Opioids Can decrease LES relaxation, worsening dysphagia Avoid or minimize use
Anticholinergics Suppress esophageal peristalsis Be aware of potential impact
Calcium Channel Blockers (CCB) Relax the LES, may temporarily relieve symptoms Can serve as bridge therapy
Nitrates Relax the LES Can serve as bridge therapy
GLP-1 Receptor Agonists May delay gastric emptying Consider pre-operative discontinuation timing
PPIs Do not affect esophageal motor function Safe to use; may be needed post-POEM

Pre-operative medication considerations: - Report all medications to the physician before treatment - Certain medications may affect HRM results and may need to be discontinued before testing (per physician instructions) - GLP-1 receptor agonists (e.g., semaglutide), due to delayed gastric emptying, may need to be discontinued in advance before general anesthesia

Q10: Long-term risk of esophageal cancer in achalasia patients?

Risk data: - Achalasia patients have a 10–50 fold increased risk of esophageal squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) compared to the general population (but the baseline incidence is very low) - Estimated long-term (20–30 year) cumulative risk is approximately 1–3% - Risk may be related to long-term food retention and chronic inflammation in the esophagus - Whether post-POEM GERD increases adenocarcinoma risk awaits long-term data

Surveillance recommendations: - Upper endoscopy every 1–3 years after treatment is recommended - Focus on esophageal mucosal changes (inflammation, metaplasia, dysplasia) - Post-POEM, also monitor GERD-related mucosal changes (Barrett’s esophagus) - If dysplasia is detected, more intensive surveillance and management are warranted

Treatment Technical Details

Q11: Anterior vs posterior approach for POEM?

Category Anterior Approach Posterior Approach
History The originally adopted approach Later developed as an alternative
Anatomical features Tunnel along the anterior esophageal wall Tunnel along the posterior wall (greater curvature side)
Gastric extension Extends along the anterior gastric cardia Extends toward the gastric fundus
Post-operative GERD Some studies suggest possibly slightly higher Some studies suggest possibly slightly lower (controversial)
Salvage after LHM May be limited (LHM uses anterior myotomy) Preferred choice (avoids anterior scar tissue)
Technical difficulty Comparable Comparable

Current consensus: - Anterior and posterior approaches have broadly comparable efficacy and safety - Either approach is acceptable for initial POEM - For patients with prior LHM, the posterior approach is recommended - Some operators choose a specific approach based on personal experience and preference

Q12: Intraoperative management of POEM complications?

Intraoperative Complication Incidence Immediate Management
Mucosal perforation 2–5% Endoscopic clips for repair; usually does not affect prognosis
Capnoperitoneum 5–15% Needle decompression of the abdomen
Pneumothorax 1–3% Thoracentesis or chest tube drainage
Subcutaneous emphysema 5–10% Usually self-resolving; decompression if severe
Bleeding 1–2% Endoscopic hemostasis (coagulation, hemoclips)
Tunnel disorientation < 1% Reorientation or procedure abandonment

Prevention of gas-related intraoperative complications: - Use CO2 rather than air for insufflation (CO2 is absorbed more rapidly) - Continuous intraoperative monitoring of abdominal cavity pressure - Anesthesia team monitors respiratory parameters for changes - Needle decompression equipment should be readily available

Q13: How to assess whether POEM myotomy length is adequate?

Intraoperative assessment methods:

Method Description Pros and Cons
Direct endoscopic visualization Observe whether muscle fibers are completely divided More subjective
Intraoperative FLIP Measure whether EGJ-DI reaches target (typically > 6 mm²/mmHg) Objective, real-time feedback, but requires additional equipment
Post-operative TBE Degree of barium emptying improvement Can only be assessed post-operatively
Post-operative HRM Degree of IRP reduction Requires waiting for post-operative recovery

Myotomy length recommendations: - Esophageal side: at least 6 cm (for Type III, can be extended to 10–15 cm to cover the spastic segment) - Gastric side: extend 2–3 cm beyond the EGJ - Intraoperative FLIP is increasingly becoming an important adjunctive tool for assessing myotomy adequacy

Q14: Anesthetic considerations specific to achalasia patients?

Consideration Details Recommendation
Aspiration risk Food retention in the esophagus increases aspiration risk NPO for at least 12–24 hours pre-op; consider pre-operative endoscopic clearance of residual food
Rapid Sequence Induction (RSI) Reduces aspiration risk during induction Anesthesia team should consider RSI
Intraoperative ventilation CO2 insufflation during POEM may impair diaphragmatic excursion Closely monitor respiratory parameters; have decompression equipment ready
Positioning POEM is typically performed in supine position Attend to pressure injury prevention
Post-operative antiemetics Prevent post-operative nausea and vomiting (which may increase esophageal pressure) Multimodal antiemetic prophylaxis

Q15: Future directions in achalasia treatment?

Research Direction Current Status Potential Impact

Robot-assisted POEM Experimental stage May improve precision

Biomarkers Under investigation Early diagnosis, prediction of treatment response
